Shaw QWERTY

Layout for typing in Shavian Alphabet /Àà Ée…™vi…ôn/.
The Unshifted and Shifted layers (Layers 1 & 2) are compatible with the Phonemic layouts, so if you've already learned to type with any Phonemic layout from this website and you decide to learn the Shavian Alphabet, you'll already know the positions of the phonemes on the keyboard.
Layers 3 & 4 contain additional punctuation marks, hyphens, etc., that may also be very useful.

The Shavian Alphabet is one of the best (if not ‚Äúthe best‚Äù) alternative writing systems for English language. It is fully phonemic (phoneme to grapheme ratio is 1:1). Moreover, the Shavian Alphabet has 6 ligatures for R-colored vowels, totaling 48 letters to represent all sounds used in English language.

The Shavian Alphabet is named after George Bernard Shaw (1856-1950), an Irish playwright, critic, an political activist. Shaw was a critic of English orthography, he himself used Pitman shorthand, a phonetic shorthand system, in writing his plays. Shaw considered the use of the Latin alphabet for writing English as a great waste of time, energy and paper, so in his will he left a provision to fund the design of a new English alphabet that would be fully phonemic.

Shaw set three main criteria for the new alphabet:

  1. at least 40 letters;
  2. as "phonetic" as possible (that is, letters should have a 1:1 correspondence to phonemes); and
  3. distinct from the Latin alphabet to avoid the impression that the new spellings were simply "misspellings".

In 1958, eight years after Shaw's death in 1950, the competition took place to design such an alphabet and Kingsley Read's system was chosen as the winner out of the 467 entries. In 1962 the book Androcles and the Lion was published, a side-by-side English and Shavian transcription. Between 1963 and 1965, 8 issues of the quarterly journal, Shaw-script, were published by Read. However, the Shavian Alphabet failed to gain popularity, and in 1966 Kingsley Read published Quikscript, aka ‚ÄúRead Alphabet‚Äù. Which also failed to gain popularity.

In 1997, Ross DeMeyere published on his website (now defunct but still can be found in archive.org) font file ShawRough, and a keymap showing the translation of Latin to Shaw alphabet. Later, in 1999, he published two probably the most popular Shavian fonts at the time: Androcles and Ghoti.

Those first font files used a clever trick: replacing Lating letters of English alphabet with Shavian symbols. Typing in Shavian was actually done with a regular QWERTY layout. That was the basis for the Shaw QWERTY keyboard layout.

In April 2003 Shavian was added to the Unicode Standard at block U+10450‚ÄìU+1047F in Plane 1 (Supplementary Multilingual). Since then many new Shavian fonts appeared with Shavian symbols in their own Unicode range.

In 2012 the first Shaw QWERTY keyboard layout for Windows was published by Brian Wojtczak aka Astrolox on his website (now defunct, but can be found in archive.org). Unfortunately he posted only the 32-bit version. However, since then many new keyboard layouts for Windows and macOS appeared.

Currently the easiest way to start typing in Shavian Alphabet on any operating system (Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, etc) is by installing Keyman. However, Keyman is a big (over 200 MB) application, with some issues like sporadically switching layouts without any user action, connecting to the Internet and sending some data despite of disabled auto-update, etc.
The best choice is to create or install an open-source keyboard layout that was designed for the OS.

All the layouts published here are open-source, published on GitHub, minimal amount of code so you can review the code yourself.

Shaw Imperial

This keyboard layout is based on Imperial Good Companion Model 6 typewriter.

Please note: This keyboard layout is not based on the Keyman Shaw Imperial.

The Imperial Model 6 typewriter:

  • had no keys ‚üÆ`‚üØ and ‚üÆ1‚üØ,
  • had no digits 0 and 1. Just like on many very old typewriters I and O had to be used instead,
  • had only capital Latin letters when "CapsLock" lever was pressed,
  • had no CapsLock button ‚Äî just like many old typewriters it had a lever to lock the SHIFT key (actually to lock the roller pin in the upper position),
  • the keys ‚üÆ3‚üØ and ‚üÆ5‚üØ had Shavian letters (IPA equivalents: ‚ü®…õ‚ü© and ‚ü®e‚ü©) which are not in official Shavian Unicode charset.   • had no letters ‚ü®êë∏,êëπ,êëø,êëº,êëΩ‚ü©.
  • had no exclamation point ‚ü®!‚ü©, no double-quote mark ‚ü®"‚ü©, no semi-colon ‚ü®;‚ü©
  • punctuation marks on the SHIFT line were: unshifted ‚ü®,¬∑.‚ü©; shifted ‚ü®?-/‚ü©

Possibly the lack of several Shavian letters and missing commonly used punctuation marks mentioned above were among the reasons why Shavian wasn't adopted back in 1970‚Äêties. That, and off course, the price of the Imperial Model 6 being about five times higher than of a regular typewriter.

Differences between this keyboard layout and the original Imperial Model 6:

  • four layers instead of only two,
  • lowercase Latin letters in the shifted layer instead of uppercase,
  • uppercase Latin letters in the AltGr layer,
  • missing Shavian letters ‚ü®êë∏,êëπ,êëø,êëº,êëΩ‚ü© added to the shifted layer in the numerals line,
  • this layout adds symbols to keys ‚üÆ`‚üØ and ‚üÆ1‚üØ:
    • ‚ü®‚∏∞‚ü© and ‚ü®¬∑‚ü© (namer dot) in the Unshifted layer
    • ‚ü®‚ó¶‚ü© and ‚ü®‚Ä¢‚ü© (bullets) in Shifted layer
    • numbers ‚ü®0‚ü© and ‚ü®1‚ü© in AltGR layer.
  • punctuation marks on the SHIFT line (unshifted layer) are ‚ü®,./‚ü© instead of originally ‚ü®,¬∑.‚ü©,
  • punctuation marks on the SHIFT line (shifted layer) are ‚ü®;:?‚ü© instead of ‚ü®?-/‚ü©,
